A Gen-Z booking experience


Role: UX lead | UI design

on-going project development.

Since 1991 Unite Student’s has been the largest provider of Purpose-built Student Accommodation (PBSA) in the UK. By 2014 a number of competitors sprung up, each pushing the boundaries of customer experience in the online space. I was part of an ambitious project to redefine the experience of matching students with their ideal accommodation at Unite Students reasserting its position as a pioneer in the PBSA space.

Initial goals and outcomes defined

The need from the business was to ease the experience of booking for our customers, reducing bounce and abandoned transactions.

The needs of our customers were a little more ambigious so I partnered with our UX researcher to gather insights from students, school leavers and parents about their priorities and motivations when searching for university accommodation.

This research was conducted to help define broad goals, and was followed up with student interviews to gain deeper qualitative understanding of their drivers, and their experience of the existing website.

User research outcomes allowed me to define broad goals for the new website booking journey.

Defining the scope

The research outcomes came with some surprises. Our working assumptions were that prospective students valued location and price most when finding accommodation, which was validated. But these priorities were equalled by user needs of comparable importance;

  1. A desire for value, both monetary but also social
  2. A desire to understand what properties were suitable for them and what terminology like 'ensuite' vs 'studio' means
  3. Anxieties and concerns about living with strangers and a desire to live among people that shared interests/similarities.

The research results also helped identify two key personas among the disparate user groups which was those that knew the type of property they wanted, and those that didn’t.

Early flows explored entry points, and how customers could intuitively match with relevant properties rather than be confronted with extensive lists.

User testing the existing platform

The users experience of the existing website revealed some key obstacles to their decision making when viewing properties. The city pages featured extensive lists of all properties available at that location with no clear indicators of their distinctive features, vicinity, or as our users requested ‘vibe’.

The unclear presentation of the existing property listings along with users confusion about them informed the first stage of design.

Wireframes explored the core exploration journey and suggested 'personal' and 'functional' questions to determine user needs.

Wireframing

The research outcomes offered some key themes to work to and three key questions informed my first foray into design

  1. How to cater to two opposing personas of ‘I know what I want’ and ‘I don’t know what I want’.
  2. How to intuitively surface relevant properties to users for exploration and comparison
  3. How to educate customers in the terminology of things like tenancy lengths and room types.

Wireframes explored the core exploration journey and suggested 'personal' and 'functional' questions to determine user needs.

Refinement and UI

Having explored comparable booking website experiences across a range of industries, I arrived at the idea of a requirements matching 'quiz' for first-time visitors and a Room Matching feature for refining property results even further. The quiz would work to assist unsure users to a desired property and was split between ‘functional’ and ‘personal’ questions.

The functional questions would educate and inform users of things like room type, tenancy length, while the personal questions were a form of data capture that could be used to pool customer types and batch bookings by flat, block and floor in a given property based on their personal preferences and personalities.

The 'Room Match' would be an intuitive customer-first feature of prompts and filters that, rather than confronting a user with an incomprehensible list of properties, would filter properties based on their preferences, matching them to a refined list that matched their requirements most closely.



Building a design system

Prior to starting work on the new website I built and documented the companies first Design System, with corresponding component libraries in Figma. This brought Unite Students a level of digital maturity where UI design could be efficient and consistent, relating back to a source of truth and governance.



Atomic design principles were employed when building an asset library beginning with simple global elements and building them into more complex features.

Assets for the property matching results page were built up accounting for different devices and mobile viewports.

Design system & UI design testing

Having built a design system and a figma component library of atomic elements prior to this project I was able to quickly develop wireframes into high fidelity UI.

I built out the room discovery flow as an interactive prototype which was peer reviewed among colleagues and stakeholders in preparation for a second round of user testing.

In early stages we pushed the UI conceptually far, which was revised down in stakeholder reviews to find consensus with the brand.

The design system helped rapidly develop wireframes into high fidelity prototypes that we could test and compare with students to validate journeys and designs.

User testing 2

The second round of user testing sought to prove the efficacy of the customer-first Room Match hypothesis, as well as validate the intuitiveness of layout and UI design by comparing it with both the legacy website and competitor websites.

The research outcomes showed a near unanimous preference for the new UI design, though some pain points for the customer-first flow were identified such as placement of the search bar and placement of filter options which were revised before entering development.

The UX researcher conducted a 2nd round of testing which helped finesse our approach to UI design and layout

MVP, fast-follows and post-MVP

Amends were made based on the second round of user testing and the designs converged to a point of readiness for refinement with our development team. Here, some features were stripped back, relegated to 'fast-follow' or post-MVP status.

For example, the 'personality' preference questions required further back-end work to build in data end-points and infrastructure to support it, which could not be accommodated in time for launch. Conversely the 'functional' questions were more easily catered for with existing data and thus became the focus of the initial launch room exploration.

The core exploration journey was supported by ancilliary pages ranging from city and property information to FAQs and blogs.
