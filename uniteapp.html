Work About

An App feature for maintenance and emergencies


Role: UX design | UI design


Certain project details may have been omitted or obfuscated to comply with
on-going project development.

The Unite Students app is a key benefit for customers choosing to live with the provider while at university. As part of the companies digital transformation project it was re-branded and had a range of features added to it including maintenance and emergency reporting tools. These tools would allow users to report and monitor a range of issues.

Initial goals and outcomes defined

The feature needed to be developed at pace and I was brought into the project late into the discovery phase to support a junior UX designer.

Without the time to conduct formal user research prior to working on the project we instead conducted informal workshops and discussions with Unite Students' customer service teams and visited local on-site property staff/students to gain insights to common issues they had while living with us.

The outcomes from these discussions formed the foundation of our priorities and the feature architecture.

Speaking to staff and students allowed us to prioritise the flow of app services. Lock outs and maintenance requests ranked highly.

UX priorities and UI fidelity

The insights gathered from our discussions allowed us to form a hypothesis and priority order for the services and maintenance pages of the app. Key points to work to were as follows:


  1. Maintenance issues with the room/communal space were most common.
  2. Lockout requests were also recurring and were high priority.
  3. Regardless of the request type students wanted notifications/sight of the status of their requests because there are always concerns they're never being dealt with.

Atomic design was used to build out reusable design assets across app and web.

Designing the concepts

Because of time pressure and my prior work of building out a design system of global assets, we worked at higher fidelity than usual, designing to brand standards and defining our flows in figma.

The priority order of the services page was based on our earlier discussions and broadly categorised as follows:

  1. Maintenance request - Something need fixing?
  2. Lockout request - Locked out?
  3. Parcel collection query - Manage your parcels
  4. Noise complaints - Too noisy?

Services were clearly denoted in the app menu and were categorised according to our guerilla research.

Early concepts and flows were designed at fidelity owing to the speed of working with the atomic design system.

Intuitive reporting flow

Within each of these broad categories was an intuitive flow tailored to the request type. For example with noise complaints a user would report the source of the noise, add additional details and then submit.

Likewise with maintenance requests users would move through a short hierarchy of options such as location of issue, type of issue before submitting a request to the maintenance team.


Noise complaints had a set dropdown to describe to signify noise sources and could follow up with free text input.

Maintenance requests had subcategories of internal, external and communal issues. Within these the user could outline specific amenities that needed attention.

Status and wayfinding

Visibility of system status was high on our priority list for user experience, since student feedback leaned heavily towards their dissatisfaction with not knowing what was happening with their requests when manually requested.

We built in an intuitive toggle between Open requests and Closed requests allowing students to see current and past jobs. Additionally we integrated a traffic light status system they could view at a glance which displayed the status their job had been set to by the property maintenance team, giving them an indication of when their issues would be resolved.

Research validation

Designs were validated with key stakeholders and built into interactive prototypes. Our researcher was able to source 5 student participants to validate the clarity and usefulness of the services feature prior to it being taken into MVP development.

Students mentioned that while the more informative status updates were an improvement on the ambiguity that exists today, they would prefer more granular/specific indications of status such as timeframes.

This validated broader stakeholder conversations of where we want to take the app services section with new infrastructure being introduced as part of the companies digital transformation projects.

The services feature deployed along with the app rebrand and NPS scores offered insight into its performance. There has been a net positive response along with a discernable drop in complaints/phonecalls to property and customer service teams, preventing bottlenecks that previously occured with these important business functions.

Usability tests with participants validated the designs.
