Lloyds Bank Academy is a learning platform helping people develop digital skills. It's committed to upskilling and connecting the UK with learning material that is accessible, engaging and available to everyone. I was embedded with Lloyds Bank from the outset to help them define the scope, audience and desired goals of the platform.

The learning platform was launched in Manchester and later rolled out for across the country for accessible digital learning.

About the project

I was embedded with Lloyds from the outset to help them define the scope, audience and desired goals of the platform. Overseeing the discovery, design and deployment phases of the initial release, my team continued to work on this organic, evolving platform as new content and features are developed to respond to existing and new audience needs.

A broad process of discovery took place in which prospective audiences were considered; their initial awareness of the platform through to completion. Existing learning platforms and apps were explored to see how certain features such as onboarding and reward systems were used to encourage use and progression.

UX & Design challenges

The initial challenge was defining the right audience. From the outset it was important to ascertain at what level the digital learning would be pitched and how audiences without digital access would discover it. Another crucial decision was how learning would be surfaced and tailored to users. Would learning be curated based on user interests, or user skill level? Each of these approaches had pros and cons, and was subject to an enduring process of iterative discovery, testing and design.

A series of proto-personas were developed to summarise core user types found in initial discovery. Their needs, fears, and aspirations were highlighted as factors the platform needed to address.

Discovery

During the discovery phase I worked with Job Centres, government data, and Lloyds customer research to understand the demographics and challenges faced by those without basic digital competencies. Research was also conducted into existing learning platforms and apps, to ascertain the strengths, weaknesses and established design patterns of online learning that could be adapted to a rudimentary audience.

Early flows explored entry points, and a users journey through the platform to completion, depending on whether they were working in a face-to-face classroom session, or autonomously via the website.

Defining the scope

Discovery made it clear that a highly empathetic, accessible approach to the experience was required for those with little prior experience of digital tools. It was also evident that an offline component to the platform delivery would be necessary for those with no digital experience, or without access to a computer/the internet, and so a face-to-face learning curriculum was approved and developed in tandem to the platform with a learning-content provider to accommodate these users. Our initial insights also showed that the platform should meet AAA accessibility standards, and be defined by simplicity as a rule, since a distinct segment of our user-base comprised elderly, vulnerable and disabled users.

Based on our insights a simplistic, clearly sign-posted learning platform that featured, modular, non-linear content was conceived. A modular structure would permit users a tailored 'bitesize learning' experience that would not prove too overwhelming or exhaustive per session. We worked with learning content providers to develop a curriculum based on both the UK Government's EDS (Essential Digital Skills), and findings from our user research their priorities (e.g. job seeking, and connecting with family).

As concepts came to fruition we created clickable prototypes with greyscale wireframes to communicate concepts, UX flows and rudimentary UI with clients.

Wireframing

The project team entered a process of iterative design - sketching ideas and rapidly prototyping to refine the experience and ease-of-use. I then developed a series of low-fidelity grayscale wireframes to workshop as InVision prototypes.

UI design was faithful to the Lloyds Bank brand, with a focus on clarity, simplicity and accessibility.

UI design

From the outset of the project branding and design was discussed and I frequently collaborated with the projects Creative Director to define avenues for design early on. Following LBG brand guidelines, a palette was developed that was minimal and task-orientated, but maintained faithfulness to the identity of the bank. It was also crucial that the design not complicate or get in the way of the learning activities in the platform so a commitment to simplicity was followed with minimal imagery or obtrusive patterns.

A series of templates were developed applying the brand styles to the wireframes; which were once again developed into clickable prototypes. The result was a clear and concise journey for the user through to relevant learning.

Deployment

The first phase of the Academy was trialled in Manchester in face-to-face classroom sessions with a sample of job seekers and relevant users. Reception to the trial was positive and the Academy was subsequently launched and greenlit for a phase 2, which is developing additional curriculums for business learning and charity learning. Lloyds Bank Academy continues to develop - now partnering with content providers such as Google and Upskill Digital to meet demands for new and existing users.
